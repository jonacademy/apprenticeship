Understanding UK Content Creator Apprenticeship:
Off-the-Job Training

In the UK, "off-the-job" training for an apprenticeship, including the Content Creator apprenticeship, is a mandatory component and refers to learning activities undertaken outside of the apprentice's normal day-to-day work duties. The key is that this training must be directly relevant to the apprenticeship standard and lead to the achievement of the knowledge, skills, and behaviours (KSBs) required for the role.

While it's "off-the-job," it doesn't necessarily mean "off-site." It can take place at the employer's premises, a college, a training provider's venue, or even through online learning, as long as it's not part of their usual daily tasks.

For a full-time apprentice, this typically means a minimum of 6 hours per week dedicated to off-the-job training, averaged over the duration of the apprenticeship. For part-time apprentices, this is pro-rata, but still must be at least 20% of their working hours.

What Counts as Off-the-Job Training?

Formal Learning & Instruction:

  • Lectures, seminars, and workshops: Delivered by the training provider, covering theoretical aspects of content creation.
  • Online learning/E-learning modules: Structured courses, interactive modules, or virtual classrooms related to content creation principles, tools, and strategies.
  • Role-playing and simulation exercises: Practicing content creation scenarios, client interactions, or crisis management in a controlled environment.
  • Training to use specialist equipment or software: Learning how to operate cameras, editing software (video, audio, image), content management systems (CMS), or design tools.

Practical Application & Development (beyond daily duties):

  • Work-based projects: Dedicated time for projects that go beyond the scope of their regular job, designed to develop new skills outlined in the apprenticeship standard. For a Content Creator, this could involve:
    • Developing a complete content strategy for a new campaign.
    • Producing a complex video series.
    • Designing and implementing a new content management system.
  • Shadowing and mentoring: Observing experienced colleagues or being mentored to learn new techniques or approaches not typically encountered in their routine tasks.
  • Industry visits or conferences: Attending events to gain a broader understanding of the content creation industry, emerging trends, and networking.
  • Time spent writing assignments and projects: Dedicated time for coursework, evaluations, reports, or portfolio development related to the apprenticeship.
  • Research: In-depth research into new content trends, audience analytics, competitor analysis, or specific content formats, beyond what's needed for immediate work tasks.
  • Creating mock content: Developing content for hypothetical briefs to practice new skills or experiment with different styles.

Personal & Professional Development:

  • Coaching sessions: Dedicated sessions with an apprenticeship coach or mentor to review progress, set goals, and discuss learning.
  • Writing reflective journals: Documenting learning and applying it to their development.
  • Learning support: Time spent receiving additional support for their learning.
  • Continuing Professional Development (CPD) activities: Engaging in activities that enhance their skills and knowledge relevant to the content creator role, even if not directly part of the formal curriculum (e.g., specific webinars on new social media algorithms, advanced SEO techniques).

What DOES NOT Count as Off-the-Job Training?

  • Normal daily duties and responsibilities: Any work that is part of their regular job role, even if it involves content creation.
  • Progress reviews or on-programme assessments: Meetings to discuss their overall apprenticeship progress or formal assessments of their KSBs.
  • Training that takes place outside the apprentice's paid working hours: Unless the apprentice is compensated for this time or takes time off in lieu.
  • English and maths (Functional Skills) training (if required): While essential for some apprentices, this is generally funded separately and does not count towards the minimum off-the-job training hours.
  • Initial assessments or onboarding activities: The very first steps of joining the apprenticeship.
  • Training not directly relevant to the apprenticeship standard: Activities that don't contribute to the specific KSBs for a Content Creator.
  • Time spent repeating failed end-point assessments: This additional training does not count towards the initial off-the-job requirement.

The key principle is that off-the-job training must deliver new learning that directly contributes to the apprentice achieving the knowledge, skills, and behaviours outlined in the Content Creator apprenticeship standard. It should be planned and agreed upon by the apprentice, employer, and training provider in the training plan.
