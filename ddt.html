Content Creator: OTJ/DDT Guide

The "New Learning" Rule: Off-the-Job (OTJ) training is time spent during work hours learning the skills needed for your qualification. To count, you must be doing something new or different that helps you meet the official Knowledge, Skills, and Behaviours (KSBs).

1. Creative Tools & Software

Learning how to use these tools for the first time counts as OTJ. Choose the one that fits your budget:

  • Video Editing: Learning Adobe Premiere Pro PAID or CapCut / DaVinci Resolve FREE.
  • Graphic Design: Learning Adobe Photoshop PAID or Canva / Photopea FREE.
  • Audio Production: Mastering Adobe Audition PAID or Audacity FREE.

2. Planning & Strategy (The "Brain" Work)

  • Storyboarding & Scripting: Learning how to draw out a video plan before filming.
    (A storyboard is like a comic-strip version of your video to show the camera angles.)
  • User Journey Mapping: Learning how a customer goes from seeing an ad to buying a product.
    (This means planning every step a person takes on your website or social media.)
  • Commercial Awareness: Learning about Budgets and how your content makes money for the business.

3. Legal, Ethics & Accessibility

  • Inclusive Content: Learning how to add Alt-Text (descriptions for the blind) and Captions (for the deaf).
  • Copyright & GDPR: Understanding who owns the music you use and how to keep people's private info safe.
  • Ethics: Learning the rules about "Sponsored Content" and being honest with your audience.

✅ This Counts as OTJ

  • New Skill: Using AI (like ChatGPT) to help brainstorm 10 new content ideas.
  • Data Analysis: Spending an hour looking at why a post "flopped" and planning how to fix it.
  • Mentoring: A senior designer showing you how to pick the right "Brand Colours."
  • Research: Looking at what your competitors are doing on YouTube.

❌ This DOES NOT Count

  • Basic Posting: Just hitting 'publish' on a post you've already finished.
  • Email & Admin: Checking your inbox or organizing your computer folders.
  • Repetitive Work: Doing the exact same edit you do every single Monday.
  • Lunch Breaks: Training must happen during your "working" time.

How to Write a Great Log Entry

Always mention what you learned and how it helps the business:

Skill/Knowledge: Accessibility & Inclusive Language (S8, K14)

What I did: I spent 90 minutes learning how to use 'Closed Captions' and 'Alt-Text' on our company's Instagram and LinkedIn posts.

Why it matters: By making our content accessible, we are making sure people with visual or hearing impairments can engage with us. This increases our reach and ensures the business is acting ethically and legally.

Top Tip: If you're using a tool for the first time—whether it's free or paid—it's learning. Log it!
